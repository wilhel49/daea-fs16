Kom Abu Bello

Old Kingdom - Greco-Roman period

Kory S Kennedy

Introduction

About 70km North of Cairo in the Western Delta lies the archaeological site of Kom Abu Bello. The site today lies partly beneath the modern village of Tarrana but in the Greco-Roman period the site was the location of the important trading city known as Terenouthis. Terenouthis, ideally placed near the Rosetta branch of the Nile in the Western Delta and situated along an important trading route for wine and salt made the city a significant urban center. The city is long gone, but the vestiges of that city remain buried in this location, perhaps most important of which is the necropolis. This necropolis contains burials from the Old Kingdom through the Greco-Roman Period, with the latter being by far the most dominant period represented. This site is known to be one of the richest in terms of Greco-Roman funerary finds in all of Egypt. These Greco-Roman burials have produced fantastic stellae and a wide variety of grave goods. Also present at the site are the remains of a temple to Hathor, sizable enough to be seen from satellite imagery as well as other site features of which have not yet been fully explained.


Regional and Geographic Setting

Kom Abu Bello lies about 70km north of Cairo in the Western Delta, situated between the Western Desert and the Rosetta Branch of the Nile. Today the modern El Nasseri Canal cuts directly through the site. The necropolis and the ancient city, covering a sizable area, are covered in part by modern land use. The villages of Tarrana, and Nasr Moustafa both cover different portions of the site and agriculture covers more. Modern land use continues to encroach upon the site but significant portions of the necropolis area west of the canal remain unoccupied (Dhennin 2011). The numerous small mounds, piles of eroding mud brick, pottery sherds, and clear indication of material remains in these open areas identify the site for what it is. This area during Greco-Roman period was important in the wine and salt trade routes and would have dominated the Western Delta developing Terenouthis into a sizable and important city. As such, the associated ruins and remains of both the city and the necropolis are varied and spread over a wide area.


Description

As previously stated, the site covers a significantly large area if one includes the city of Terenouthis, the necropolis, and the associated areas which relate to periods prior to the Greco-Roman period which Kom Abu Bello is primarily known for. But the site can be fairly easily described by its various sections and major features. The city of Terenouthis lies on the east side of the El Nasseri Canal. The encroachment of modern life and agriculture have significantly reduced the size of the site available for excavation here, but parts of the ancient city are easily identifiable by the uneven ground and mounds of mud brick. Evidence of the industrial production of amphora, potentially for salt can be seen on the surface. The city street grid can still be detected, at least from aerial imagery (Mefkat, Kom Abou Billou). Just west of the ancient city, the El Nasseri Canal cuts across the site in a North/South direction. The Terrana bridge crosses the canal and leads one directly to the necropolis. The necropolis could be further divided into sections according to period. Generally it seems the northern parts of the necropolis are from earlier periods with Old and New Kingdom burials being found along the northern part of the canal (Hawass 1979). The Greco-Roman burials dominate the area directly west of the bridge. Also in this area lies the very large structure believed to be a temple to Hathor. The structure is large enough that it can be seen from satellite imagery. This area also is easily identifiable as an archaeological site from the mounds and piles of eroding mud brick as well as scattered sherds.




Excavations

Portions of Kom Abu Bello have been excavated by different expeditions since the close of the 19th century. Most of these excavations unfortunately were inadequately documented or unpublished (Dhennin 2011). The first such excavation occurred in 1887 and 1888 under the Egyptian Exploration Fund, headed by F.L. Griffith. This excavation resulted in the discovery of the temple to Hathor in the western section of the necropolis.A second very short expedition headed by E. Peterson for the University of Michigan was conducted in 1935. The next major period of excavation at the site began in 1969. The construction of the El Nasseri Canal was laid out in a path directly through the site and thus a salvage excavation was undertaken to preserve and document threatened sections of the site. These excavations headed by various individuals lasted for roughly eight years. Shafik Farid of the Antiquities Department headed the first round of excavations. His excavation covered an area of about 4km by 170m within the necropolis (Farid 1973). In 1970 the excavations continued under the supervision of Abdel Hafeez Abdel Aal of the Egyptian Antiquities organization (Abdel Aal 1983). He was followed by El Sawi from 1972-1975 and finally Zahi Hawass finished the excavations in 1975-1976 (Hawass 1979). They apparently uncovered a Temple to Apollo in the northern section of the site but this has been difficult to verify (Hawass 2005). These excavations constitute the bulk of what has been recovered up until very recently. Some criticism has been aimed at the quality of documentation and lack of publishing from these excavations but as it was a salvage dig ahead of a public project, it is understandable that the teams likely had time and resource pressures. These excavations recovered burials ranging from the 6th Dynasty, Old Kingdom through the 4th Century AD (Greco-Roman period) (Hawass 1979). They apparently uncovered a Temple to Apollo in the northern section of the site but this has been difficult to verify (Hawass 2005) (Dhennin 2011). More than 6,000 tombs were uncovered during this period, mostly from the Greco-Roman period but did include some from Pharaonic times. More recently excavations have been undertaken by the French Institute of Archaeology- Caior in concert with the University of Lille under Sylvain Dhennin(Dhennin 2011). These excavations have been undertaken to get a better understanding of the overall site as well as presumably to fill in the gaps created by lack of publishing and the poor documentation of previous excavations. The site continues to be excavated today by the IFAO and University of Lille and much of the visual material and modern interpretation of Kom Abu Bello will likely come from this expedition.


Results

The excavations of Kom Abu Bello have recovered a wide range of materials from a variety of periods and contexts. However the most thoroughly investigated elements of Kom Abu Bello have been in the Greco-Roman necropolis. Thus far, there have been somewhere around 6,000 to 8,000 burials uncovered, though the incomplete documentation and lack of publishing makes it difficult to verify exact numbers(Dhennin 2011). The burials within the necropolis come in a wide variety but by far the most common are burials beneath a superstructure of mud brick. The body is placed in a sand pit,generally facing west, subsequently covered, and a mud-brick superstructure erected on top of the burial pit. The structures come in a wide variety of styles including rectangular, oblong, round, and pyramid shaped styles. Some of these are unique within Egypt, only found at Kom Abu Bello. Within the superstructures, usually on the east side, is a small niche in which a funerary stelle is placed. Another smaller niche a small offering table is positioned in front of this. Some of the superstructures appear to be related to one another with offering tables possibly associated with multiple tombs facing each other(Dhennin 2011). More than 450 stellae have been recovered from Kom Abu Bello. They are probably mass produced but are generally of good quality and can be found in many museums throughout the world(Hawass 1979). Other burials have been recorded between the superstructures, some in sarcophagi or in wooden coffins. It has been suggested by Sylvain Dhennin that the mortuary practices exhibited at Kom Abu Bello may be a mix of Egyptian and Greek or Roman practices(Dhennin 2011).. Also recovered from Kom Abu Bello are other items including coins dating from the 2nd to the 4th century sometimes placed in the hands of the deceased (Abdel Aal 1983). oil lamps also commonly placed with the deceased as well as, bracelets, earrings, finger rings, pottery and glass vessels, amulets and beads (Farid 1973). There is also one notable burial containing 50 individuals, all with traces of violent death, including embedded arrowheads. Hawass suggests that a battle may have been fought in the vicinity(Hawass 1979). In regard to the other sections of the site, the urban area of Terenouthis and the larger temple structures at Kom Abu Bello have either not been adequately studied or the available data is inadequate to make accurate conclusions. Much work remains to be done at the site(Dhennin 2011).


Conclusion

Kom Abu Bello really is a fascinating site that gets overlooked in Egyptian archaeology. This is possibly due to the fact that the site is from the less popular Greco-Roman period or because the site is not well understood or documented. Either way, it is likely there will be significant discoveries made there as excavations move forward. The city remains to be excavated, which in time will likely reveal interesting information about Egyptian life in the Greco-Roman period. In fact Sylvain Dhennin has suggested that simply defining the period as Greco-Roman is inadequate and moving forward we can better pinpoint time-spans within the period(Dhennin 2011).. The site is still under study by the Institute of French Archaeology-Cairo in partnership with the University of Lille today.
