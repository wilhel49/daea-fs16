el-Adaïma

Predynastic to Early Dynastic

Kailyn Williams

Introduction

el-Adaïma is a necropolis of the predynastic time period, which was between the middle and the end of the fourth millennium. It is an important archaeological site in southern Egypt, filled with series of tombs and a perfectly preserved children’s cemetery. Many archaeologists have excavated the site because it is rich in artifacts that give us a glimpse into how life was for those who lived there in the past. Excavations of the site suggest that the area was once a very lively, industrious area filled with many people.


Location

el-Adaïma is located along the west bank of the Nile, 60 km south of Luxor (25°14′56″N, 32°34′43″E) and 30 km north of Hierakonpolis. The site occupies part of a rise with an elevation of up to 5 m above the surrounding floodplain.


Description of Site

The site consists of a badly plundered cemetery called the “Western Cemetery”; an intact cemetery, containing mainly children’s graves, called the “Eastern Cemetery”; and a settlement, which appears as a large area with artifacts scattered over the surface, extending for about 1 km along the edge of the cultivated land. The site covers about 40 ha (settlement: 35 ha; cemeteries: 5 ha). In all, 881 graves were discovered and excavated. From a chronological point of view, the Western Cemetery is older; its use is recorded from the Naqada IC period until Naqada IIID–Dynasty 3. While burial continued in the Western Cemetery, the first graves were dug in the southern part of the Eastern Cemetery in the Naqada IIC–IID transition period and continued until Dynasty 3. This indicates that, there was activity across the site spanning from Naqada IC to the end of Dynasty 3 (3700–2600 BC)


Discussion of Excavations

The site was discovered and partly excavated in 1908 by Jacques de Morgan, and then rediscovered by Fernand Debono in 1973. In the winter for 1906-1907 while conducting surveys for the Brooklyn Museum, Henri de Morgan located Adaïma. De Morgan returned the following field season (1907-1908) to continue surveying the settlement area and to conduct excavations within the two cemeteries he had previously pinpointed. By the close of the season, de Morgan had investigated a total of fourteen burials. The artifacts collected from the 1907-1908 season have been stored at the Musée d’Archéologie nationale in Saint-Germain-en-Laye as well as the Brooklyn Museum New York. In 1973, Fernando Debono began his additional excavations at Adaïma. Unlike Morgan, he focused his attention on only one of the cemeteries rather than the settlement and second cemetery. In the cemetery thirty burials were uncovered—however, Debono was not the first individual to expose the burials. A majority of those investigated appeared to have been robbed and plundered by looters during the Predynastic period. In response to the site of Adaïma’s preservation being threatened by sebakh and other agricultural activities, excavations have been occurring off and on from 1989 to 2005. Rescue measures were put into place by the director of the Institut franҫais d’archéologie ortientale, P. Posener Krieger, in conjunction with the Department of Antiquities. Once the site had been surveyed once again, large scale excavations took place in both the cemeteries as well as the settlement from 1996 to 2000. The cemetery and burial investigations were conducted by Éric Crubézy and Thierry Janin while the settlement studies were operated by Nathalie Buchez and Béatrix Midant-Reynes So far, 881 graves have been excavated in addition to mortars, hearths, black-topped wares, and living floors. The most recent excavations were carried out from 1989 until 2005 under the direction of Béatrix Midant-Reynes as part of the activities of the French Institute of Archaeology in Cairo.


Results and significant excavations

Recent topographic and chronological analytical results based on a study of ceramics show the stages of development of the site: During Naqada I-IIB/IIC, there was a massive expansion in the settlement. Many people lived there and the population continued to grow and expand for over two centuries. Houses and different types of dwellings were being created rapidly for families to live. With the addition of houses and dwellings, cemeteries had to be made for the area. Excavations indicate that there was no specialized production unit. Full development of “elite” tombs is also indicated during Naqada IIC. Naqada IIC/IID-IIA1 is characterized with a decline in population. The liveliness that evolved during Naqada I-IIB/IIC slowly vanished. Different areas were abandoned, including areas that were along the edge of the floodplain. Areas of the cemeteries were abandoned as well. Analysis of the site also indicates that children’s cemeteries present palaeo-demographic data in favor of a reduced population. Excavations of the period Naqada IIA1/IIIA2-Dynasty 3 suggest that the populations changed. The community that lived there was much smaller than that of Naqada I-IIB/IIC. It also suggests that there was possibly specialized production with implications for a large-scale production and redistribution system. Occupied from 3700 to 2600 BC, this site represents the settlement of a small community or people who had very little differences socially. The major stages in the site’s development appear to be closely correlated with those of the “urban centers” we see today. These major stages also contributed to the economic and political growth of Hierakonpolis. The decrease in population may indicate an exodus towards the “cities” such as Hierakonpolis. The result, for the Naqada III period, is the perseverance of the site in the form of a single production unit the end of its occupation. The analysis was based on the study of the entire ceramic corpus. This involved 120,000 sherds and 1240 complete vessels, of which 89 came from the settlement and 1151 from the cemeteries. All vessels deposited in graves had been used previously in domestic contexts and not all of the vessel forms used in settlement areas were found in graves. Many of the graves excavated at el-Adaïma contain very well preserved skeletons, representing the people who once inhabited the area. In all, 881 graves yielding 800 morphologically well-preserved skeletons were excavated and studied by osteoarchaeologists and anthropobiologists. Excavations of the site and analysis of the remains have suggested that the people of Adaima laid very young children to rest inside the settlement, some of which given necessities for the afterlife and jewelry such as bracelets. The earliest internment of six people (one adult, one adolescent, and four children) was prepared around 3700/3600 BCE, about the time that the first people started settling at Adaïma. The tomb (S55) was set on top of a small mound with no archaeological remains were discovered in the area. All individuals of S55 died and were buried at the same time so that the tomb was considered as the ‘original first tomb’. After, the Western Cemetery was then used for the next 600 to 700 years as a final resting place. In one case, extraordinary care was taken over the placement of the bodies creating an image of love expressed by the funerary party as much as a gesture of tenderness between and woman and a child. In burial S11 the body of the child was place in such a way that the child’s neck was resting on the woman’s arm while her fingertips were touching the child’s forehead. Members of the elite were not buried with the general population who were lower in status. The Eastern Cemetery is divided into a northern and southern section by a gap formed by an old wadi of around 15m width. The southern section contained only graves of children, many placed in vessels, and with dates around 3150/3300-3200 BCE, with younger tombs dating to around 3150-3050 BCE. The Eastern Cemetery was only used by a small community of 10 to 30, and archeologists have speculated that they were extended family.
