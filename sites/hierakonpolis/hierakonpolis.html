Hierakonpolis

Predynastic

Amy Hair

Introduction

Hierakonpolis, a capital city in Upper Egypt, is regarded as one of the most important predynastic sites in the region. Hierakonpolis is a site of great importance not only in the archaeological record, but also served as a center for trade, material production, and a royal residence, (Petrie 7). Hierakonpolis’ reign spanned from 3200 B.C. to the beginning of the Early Dynastic Period, (Shaw 29). Hierakonpolis, also known as the ancient city of Nekhen, and surrounding cemeteries spanned about 50 miles and included a cult center for the god Horus, (Shaw 30). Hierakonpolis was a significant site in ancient Egypt, playing a major role in trade, material production, and unifying upper and lower Egypt, (Shaw 29). At the city’s peak, the archaeological record indicates a royal presence at the ancient city of Nekhen, (Petrie 7).

Excavations at Hierakonpolis began in 1898 by James Quibell and Fredrick Green, both of whom had previously worked with Sir William Flinders Petrie, a man often credited as the father of Egyptian archaeology, (Shaw 32). Early excavations discovered some of the more notable artifacts, including the Narmer Palette, Scorpion Macehead, and the Narmer Macehead, (Shaw 32). Excavations at Hierakonpolis revealed the immense significance of this site as the capital of Upper Egypt and continue to produce valuable insight even with the most recent excavations.


Introduction to Location

Hierakonpolis is a predynastic site in Upper Egypt, located to the west of the Nile River. The site is located near and under the modern village of Muisaat, (Hoffman 16). East of the Nile river is the dynastic site El Kab, and closest major town, Edfu, is about 25 km south, (Hoffman 16). The site is located between the Eastern Desert and the eroded geology of the Western Desert, both of which are remains of the Nubian Sandstone Formation that dates back to the cretaceous geological period, (Hoffman 16).

In terms of geology, the site can be split into three distinct zones, including the alluvium, the low desert, and the Nubian Sandstone Hills. The first of the three, the alluvium, is relatively flat, but also encompasses desert hills and terraces, (Hoffman 16). This zone begins at the river and goes to the border of cultivation, and due to intense irrigation methods saline horizons are commonly witnessed, (Hoffman 17). The next zone, the low desert, is marked by the edges of cultivation, which have changed over time due to many factors including population pressure and political stability, (Hoffman 17). The is characterized by a Pleistocene silt covered by sand and debris, and the land gradually rising to the west until reaching gravel, (Hoffman 17). The most common flora includes shrubs at the wet cultivated borders, and in terms of animals, rodents and insects are most common (Hoffman 17). The last of the three zones, the Nubian Sandstone Hills, extend roughly 5 km into the Western Desert with a maximum height reaching about 40 km above the landscape, (Hoffman 18). This area is heavily intersected by wadis, that eventually drain into the the Great Wadi, (Hoffman 18).

The climate at Hierakonpolis is characteristic of the region. It is hot and dry, with temperatures gradually reaching an average of above 100'F in the summer. In the winter months temperatures averages of 60'F to about 80'F during the day and getting as low as 32'F at night, (Hoffman 20). As one of the driest regions in the world rainfall is infrequent, however, when rain does occur it tends to be extremely intense, (Hoffman 19).

Description of Site

Hierakonpolis is a predynastic site partially located under the modern village of Muisaat in Upper Egypt. The site was occupied from about 3200 – 2600 BC, reaching it’s peak during the late predynastic period to the early dynastic times, (Redford 2013). The site is associated with kingship and unification of Upper and Lower Egypt, (Redford 2013). After the dynastic period, Hierakonpolis declined to being a provincial center, due to changing environmental and political factors, (Redford 2013).

The site is split into two archaeological zones. The first of which is located in the low mounds in the flood plains, that house the remains of a town and the temple mound, (Redford 2013). The second of the two zones is a series of interrelated sites scattered across three and a half kilometers in the low desert and goes on for about two kilometers west in the a wadi of the western desert, (Redford 2013). The first excavations began from 1897 to 1899 has since then continued into modern times, (Redford 2013). The earliest excavations were conducted by James Quibell and Fredrick Green, and funded by the Egyptian Exploration Fund, (Hoffman 175).

Past and Present Excavations

Excavations at Hierakonpolis began in the late 1890s with James Quibell and Fredrick Green, and since 1967 by a handful of different institutes have been a part of intense excavations and survey expeditions. Early excavations by Quibell and Green began in 1898, and focused on a 4th millennium B.C. temple, that was contemporary with emergence of Egyptian civilization, (Shaw 32). These excavations revealed some of the more prevalent artifacts discovered include the Narmer Palette and Macehead and the Scorpion Macehead in what is called the main deposit, which was placed between two walls that were dated to the Old Kingdom, (Shaw 32). However, the Narmer Palette it self was not found in the main deposit, it was just in the near by proximity, (Shaw 32). The main deposit also yielded hundreds of plain mace heads, animal figures in green pottery, ivory statuettes, plaques, and elaborate plates and cups associated with royal names were discovered, (Petrie 7). Adjacent to this main deposit, a series of storerooms were discovered near by statues of the 2nd Dynasty pharaoh, Khasekhemwy, and thus provides the earliest example of Egyptian sculpture in the third dimension, (Shaw 32). Excavations that took place a year later in 1899 revealed another one of the more popular artifacts at Hierakonpolis, Tomb 100, other wise know as the ‘Painted Tomb,’ (Shaw 33). The tomb, discovered by Green, while damaged, the southwestern wall showed painting dating stylistically back to the 4thmillium B.C. and included images of boat, religious symbols, and hunting and trapping scenes, (Shaw 34).

Moving into the twentieth century, excavations at Hierakonpolis continued to reveal valuable artifacts and information regarding Predynastic and early Dynastic Egyptian society. The Metropolitan Museum of Art, who have for a long time shown interest in excavating a predynastic site, began excavations at Hierakonpolis in 1933 and continued through 1944, (Lansing 37). Their excavations unearthed elaborate pots with depictions of birds and trees, and also showed the use of colored minerals, thought to possibly to have had healing powers, around the eyes, (Lansing 47). In 1958 Michael Hoffman worked on locality HK29, in which a clay paved court yard was discovered dated to the Naqada II period, (Shaw 22). Excavations of this locality also unearthed religious architecture and masses of faunal remains, ranging from goats to crocodiles, and is thought to have suggest ritual slaughter, (Shaw 33). From 1967 forward a number of different excavators and institutions have worked at Hierakonpolis, including Walkter Fairervis and Michael Hoffman, and is still currently under modern excavation, (Shaw 34). The Fairervis and Hoffman excavations sought to understand environmental and cultural aspects of the site, (Shaw 34). Later on in the 1908’s Hoffman also excavated Locality 6, about 700 x 300 feet, which included 11 graves, eight of which were elite individuals, the rest consisted of cattle, baboons, and dogs, (Shaw 34).

Significance of Excavations

Hierakonpolis has been seen as a vital site in demonstrating predynastic culture and unification of Upper and Lower Egypt. Throughout excavations at Hierakonpolis it has become evident that the strategic location of the city led to trade relations with Sudan, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the mineral resources of the eastern desert, (Castillos 2). At the site preservation and evidence of settlement is much better preserved then at sites located more north, (Lansing 37). Excavation at Hierakonpolis has revealed a lot about predynastic culture, including how when summer rains decreased in the dessert, thus causing inundations of the Nile river to decrease, settlement moved closer to the water, (Hoffman 73). Multiple artifacts have also indicated the importance of Hierakonpolis in the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt. Artifacts such as the Narmer Palette and Macehead and the Scorpion Macehead help to tell the story of unification in Egypt, (Shaw 34). Excavations at Hierakonpolis have also focused on mortuary customs, for example HK6, otherwise known as the Pillared Hall, which is the earliest known evidence of above ground funerary architecture, (Stevenson 433). Other evidence found at HK29A shows evidence of ritual feasting, due to the large amounts of faunal remains recovered, (Stevenson 433). More modern surveys and excavations have shown specialized craft production and have given a full picture of the city and predynastic culture that gave rise to the world’s fist nation state, (Redford 2013). Hierakonpolis is valuable in the sense that it allows for an understanding of predynastic culture and society and the process of unifying Upper and Lower Egypt.

Conclusion

Hierakonpolis is a site of great importance when looking at predynastic culture and society. The site has unearthed many artifacts pertaining to the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt, and the process of nation creation within Egypt, (Shaw 34) Located in lower Egypt along the Nile River, partially under the modern village of Muisaat, (Hoffman 16). Excavations at the site began in the late 1890s with the work of James Quibell and Fredrick Green, and have continued into modern day with the work of many excavators and institutions, (Lansing 37). Excavations at Hierakonpolis have revealed valuable artifacts, such at the Narmer Palette, Narmer Macehead, and Scorpion Macehead that have spoke to the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt, and mortuary and religious customs were revealed through excavations at Tomb 100 and Locality 6, (Shaw 34). Hierakonpolis is a valuable site that has provided treasured information about predynastic culture and society in Egypt.
